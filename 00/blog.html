The Eternal Sin

Published September 10th, 2025

Diablerie counter

This blog proposes a new mechanic for Vampire: The Eternal Struggle. Whenever a vampire commits diablerie a counter is placed on them, simply called a "diablerie counter".

The counter does not have to have any codified effects on its own. Instead, the significance of the counter can depend on future card designs which reference the counter. This proposition as such is sound, as it has no effect on its own, albeit there is a hidden cost to tracking yet another counter. Instead, how it plays out will be entirely dependent on how the cards that reference this new token or counter function.

Why?

The purpose of this proposition is to provide a mechanism to create the potential for secondary long-term consequences for diablerie, in addition to the primary and immediate consequence of the bloodhunt referendum.

Sometimes bloodhunts don't occur for the diablerist due to opportunistic and temporary reasons, or the Methuselah knows they can get away with it without repercussions relatively easily. After the referendum, there is nothing (mostly).

The current cards which reference diablerists typically have a window within which they can be played, such that it's the turn after, or during the act. It's rare enough so that it's not worthwhile to play most of the current cards that reference it, and because they don't have alternative uses.

See for example:
Subdued by the Blood,
Crimson Fury,
Sixth Tradition: Destruction,
The Deadliest Sin.

For the acting diablerist there are several cards that are played, because for the active player the frequency of this event is much more in their control, and they're mostly related to avoiding the bloodhunt. The cards for the reacting party are not commonly played.

Lore supports the persistence of diablerie consequences

In the World of Darkness and Vampire: The Masquerade vampires with Auspex can detect diablerists. Among the Camarilla, this sin is considered the ultimate crime.

The brutality of VTES can be off-putting.

Although the World of Darkness can be violent and mature, it can lead to downsides in a 5-player social game. If someone loses quickly in a 2-player game, that's fine, it's the end of the game. In a multiplayer game that can mean that one person has to leave early while the game continues, or just suffer through the rest of it out of courtesy.

The table balance guards against this kind of problem. Other players won’t let someone commit an opportunistic diablerie if it wrecks the balance, for example. This kind of guard rail is exquisite — it doesn’t feel like an arbitrary hardcoded rule, but rather is something that flourishes on the abstract level of player culture.

However, it doesn’t work always. And it should not work always, either. For example, if bloodhunt was guaranteed, there would be no referendum, and it wouldn't be interesting at all. But this can result in situations where someone by chance happens to get into a position where they can get away with diablerie for no consequence — for example, due to a temporary vote lock in the early game.

This proposition is not out to make diablerie impossible, nor is it to say the current system has a flaw. New effects don't have to all be negative. If successful, this mechanic won't be merely another arbitrary counter, bloodhunt would still be the primary consequence, it could add a little bit of extra strategy and a reason to hesitate to the decision making process, provide an opportunity for new designs, at best might on average improve the players' experience, and slightly tip the scales against amaranth.
